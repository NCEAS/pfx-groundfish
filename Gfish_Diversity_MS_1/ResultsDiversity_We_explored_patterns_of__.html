Results

Diversity

      We explored patterns of diversity in the groundfish community by examining five diversity metrics within each of the 9 shallow and 5 deep study areas in the GOA to understand how diversity varies across this large marine ecosystem.  Species richness at each study area was relatively low (across the 31 years), and ranged from approximately 9 - 13 in shallow areas and 11 - 14 in deep areas, with the largest difference between any two sites being just 4 species (Figure 2 a, b).  The western-most shallow sites (8, 9) had higher species richness ADD STATS HERE, and those sites east of Kodiak Island had lower richness (Figure).  Alpha diversity (within area diversity exp\(H\)) varied little between study areas, and averaged approximately 9 species across both shallow and deep sites (Figure 2 c, d).  Shallow sites had slightly higher alpha diversity on average, but also had higher variability ADD STATS HERE, while deep site 13, at the western end of Kodiak Island, had the lowest alpha diversity ADD STATS HERE.  Beta diversity (between area diversity) was also remarkably similar across both shallow and deep areas, and averaged approximately 9 in shallow areas and 5 in deep areas (Figure 2 e, f).  
        Given the invariance of diversity both within and between study areas, we examined functional diversity to better understand this groundfish community.  Rao's Q, our measure of functional diversity, showed little variation across most shallow and deep local communities with few exceptions (Figure 2 g, h): Shallow local community 6 differed significantly from local communities 1, 2, and 4, and local areas 2 and 9 also differed significantly (Tukey test, p < 0.05).  Deep areas did not differ significantly in functional diversity (ANOVA, p = 0.071).  Overall, species richness and diversity as well as functional diversity of the groundfish community was conserved across the central GOA over this 31-year period. 

Community composition

         In shallow study areas 1 - 7 east of Kodiak (and to a lesser degree in deep areas 1 - 4), differences in community composition were driven by rare and intermediate-density species.  Composition of the most abundant species did not show changes: transforming the data to emphasize the influence of the most abundant species did not change which species were most abundant (i.e. Hellinger transformation, Supplementary Figure S1).  Instead, changes in community composition were only apparent when data were transformed to allow subdominant species equal chance of explaining community structure (i.e. Wisconsin - Square root transformation, Figure 3).
