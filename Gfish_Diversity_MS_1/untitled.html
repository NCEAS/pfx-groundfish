Introduction

      The mechanisms driving ecosystem stability, patterns of biodiversity, and community structure remain important topics of ecological study, as demonstrated by the substantial efforts dedicated to understanding diversity-stability relationships and the processes underlying species co-existence.  However, our knowledge of these concepts stems mostly from study of local ecological systems.  Much less is known about what drives large-scale patterns of stability and diversity, especially in a spatial context. 

      Spatial stability in ecosystem properties and processes can be dependent on patterns of biodiversity at different scales, from organisms to ecosystems (Gonzalez 2009, Leibold 2004, Wang 2014, Wang 2016).  Species richness is one of the most fundamental diversity metrics and measures the number of species in a given space (habitat, ecosystem, etc.).  In a more explicitly spatial context, the effects of biodiversity are can be examined within local scale communities (\(\alpha\) diversity), between local scale communities (\(\beta\) diversity), and within regional scale communities (\(\gamma\) diversity) (CITES, Jost 2007).  Ecological theory puts forward a couple potential relationships between diversity at different scales, but the most common is a multiplicative relationship, where gamma diversity is the product of alpha and beta diversity, which confer stability through asynchronous variation within local communities and through asynchronous variation in biomass and community composition among local communities, respectively (Jost 2007, Wang 2014).  But what drives biodiversity? 

      One mechanism that can structure biodiversity is disturbance, as outlined in the Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis (Connell 1978).  In this framework, medium levels of disturbance increase local (\(\alpha\)) diversity, while low or high levels of disturbance decrease local diversity.  However, disturbance effects on diversity can not be considered independently, but must be considered together with productivity effects, which can also influence biodiversity (Huston 2014).  In high productivity areas, theory says that disturbance will increase local (\(\alpha\)) diversity, but in low productivity areas, will decrease local (\(\alpha\)) diversity (Huston 2014). 

       Identifying a single unifying construct that seems adequate to explain patterns of species co-existence and community composition is difficult (Wilson 2011, but see Gonzalez 2009).  Variation in composition between local communities (\(\beta\) diversity) can be generated by factors whose relative importance can vary among spatial scales (Leibold 2004, Declerck 2011), and may reflect stronger environmental heterogeneity or reduced exchange of organisms (hereafter dispersal) among local habitats (Legendre 2005, Cadotte 2005, Declerck 2011).  Environmental heterogeneity is a key mechanism linking the local and regional scales, and can underpin spatial patterns of diversity by creating local habitats suited to different suites of species.  Dispersal also links local and regional scales, and supports stability at the regional scale by permitting local species coexistence through enabling locally extirpated species to recolonize from neighboring local communities (cite original ref for the concept of dispersal in metacommunities) (Leibold 2004, France 2006, Mellin 2014).    Lower dispersal levels also maximize diversity and provide compensatory species dynamics, thus maximizing regional stability (Gonzalez 2009).  In these ways, drivers of community structure and spatial diversity are linked and influence ecosystem stability. 

      Variation in the relative importance of dispersal and environmental heterogeneity can generate spatial patterns in local community structure and composition (Tamme 2010, Leibold 2004, Yang 2015).  While many studies of community structure and species co-existence have been conducted in terrestrial systems, comparative studies in marine systems are more difficult and thus fewer studies have examined patterns and mechanisms structuring community composition and species co-existence in the oceans (Smith et al. 1993).  However, recent studies have supported the theory that community composition influences stability of ecosystem functions in marine ecosystems through response redundancy, because compensation among species (or functional groups) leads to more consistent ecosystem properties (Schindler et al. 2015, Blake 2010, Schindler et al. 2010).  Recent reviews also indicate that dispersal supports local coexistence of species via the spatial storage effect, where species disperse across an ecosystem in order to maintain optimal environmental conditions despite spatial variation  (Gonzalez 2009, Snyder 2004, Chesson 2004).  The mechanisms underlying co-existence and composition effects, response and functional redundancy, and dispersal, may also influence diversity across larger spatial scales. 

        The Gulf of Alaska (GOA) provides an excellent region to examine mechanisms governing ecosystem stability, biodiversity, and community composition.  This high latitude marine ecosystem is subject to large seasonal, interannual, decadal, and multi-decadal climate-driven environmental variability that can impact species and ecosystems (Anderson & Piatt 1999, Hare & Mantua 2000, Hollowed et al. 2001, Stachura et al. 2014).  Storm events, seasonal wind mixing, and longer-term shifts in climate forcing have been documented to be important factors governing this large marine ecosystem, and can serve to create heterogeneity (ex: freshwater inputs, sea surface temperatures) or homogeneity (ex: atmospheric forcing, Pacific Decadal Oscillation, Alaska coastal current) and impact communities at multiple spatial scales (Henson 2007, Gargett 1997, Bond 2004, DiLorenzo 2008, Mantua et al 19XX).  This continental shelf ecosystem also exhibits spatial heterogeneity and complex bathymetry that is punctuated by submarine troughs and canyons which act to steer currents, creating several relatively distinct areas of similar conditions that can be compared (Lagerloef 1983, Strabeno 2004).  Previous studies have utilized this complex topography for natural experiments based on comparing responses of selected fish species to natural or anthropogenic disturbance (Hollowed et al. 2007, Logerwell et al. 2007, Walline et al. 2012).   This spatial heterogeneity can also lead to spatial variation in environmental conditions that can influence ecosystem processes and functions. 

     The groundfish assemblage in the GOA is widely distributed, well-studied, readily identified to species, supports important fisheries, and most species are mobile and known to respond to perturbations (CITE Anderson & Piatt 1996, Hollowed papers, others).  These species also exhibit relatively long life spans which could preserve reproductive potential over time, but have different life history strategies.  Strategies include broadcast spawning (e.g., Walleye Pollock), protracted pelagic larval stages (e.g., Dover Sole), and larval parturition in regions of high larval retention (e.g., rockfish species).  While generally mobile, adults do exhibit differences with some species having broad feeding migrations (e.g., Pacific Cod), and others showing territorial behavior (e.g., EXAMPLE NEEDED). 




Here we apply the ecological theory outlined above to examine biodiversity, community composition, and ecosystem stability in the GOA high-latitude large marine ecosystem. 

We focused this study on evaluating spatial patterns of diversity and community composition among the 55 most common groundfish species across the GOA over a period of large natural (e.g., climate regime shifts) and anthropogenic (e.g., EVOS) environmental perturbations, and considered whether we could identify mechanisms underlying these spatial patterns (Anderson & Piatt 1999, Incardona et al. 2015).  We geostatistically modeled groundfish abundance and biomass from the Alaska Fisheries Science Center (AFSC) trawl survey, and then tested for differences in diversity, community composition, and stability across the GOA.
