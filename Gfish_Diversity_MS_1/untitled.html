     The portfolio effect states that biodiversity insures ecosystems function more reliably because multi-species communities increase the likelihood that some species will be able to maintain functioning in the face of perturbations, and is one explanation for stability in multi-species communities (Yachi & Loreau 1999, Loreau 2010).  This effect occurs when species have asynchronous responses to environmental perturbations, but when summed across the community the responses average out statistically leading to increased stability with increased diversity (Cottingham et al. 2001, Doak et al. 1998).  Thus, we expect biodiversity to increase community stability by buffering against declines in functioning or enhancing biomass production of communities with greater numbers of species or functional traits.  The specific mechanisms that underlie these effects are species' intrinsic responses to perturbations, the speed of those responses, and a reduction in interspecific competition (Yachi & Loreau 1999, Loreau & de Mazancourt 2013).     

The portfolio effect - i.e. that variation between local communities confers regional stability in biotic assemblages - can arise from several mechanisms operating at explicitly spatial scales: (i) spatial autocorrelation (and eventually spatial heterogeneity) in environmental noise or perturbations, which gives rise to spatial heterogeneity (and spatial autocorrelation) in the magnitude and/or rate of species intrinsic responses to environmental conditions (this is of primary and fundamental importance; Wang & Loreau 2014), (ii) trophic and non-trophic interactions, and behavioural responses to species interactions (also differential movement scales which allow species to 'operate' at different spatial scales), which manifest at explicitly spatial scales (e.g. competitive refugia / niche partitioning in space; Loreau & de Mazancourt 2013, Wang & Loreau 2014) ... note for when writing discussion: this will be related to our functional complementarity analysis (iii) dispersal can beget regional stability via spatial averaging (direct effect) or by promoting greater diversity (indirect effect; Loreau et al. 2003); alternatively, low levels of dispersal may maximize diversity and provide compensatory species dynamics, ultimately establishing a spatial portfolio effect.  Ultimately, ecosystem stability at the regional scale can be decomposed additively or multiplicatively into (1) variability at (ie within) local scales and (2) beta (spatial) variation, of which there are 2 components: (a) variation (uneveness) in biomass and (b) asynchronous fluctuations among local communities (Wang & Loreau 2014) ... think about how ecological processes influence each.         The Gulf of Alaska (GOA) provides an excellent region to examine mechanisms governing community composition and the portfolio effect. It exhibits complex topography, being punctuated by submarine troughs and canyons which act to steer currents, resulting in several relatively distinct areas of similar conditions that can be compared (Lagerloef 1983).  Previous studies have utilized this complex topography for natural experiments based on comparing responses of selected fish species to natural or anthropogenic disturbance (Hollowed et al. 2007, Logerwell et al. 2007, Walline et al. 2012).   Boreal marine systems such as the GOA experience high levels of seasonal, interannual, decadal and multi-decadal variability which in turn impact marine species (Anderson & Piatt 1999, Hare & Mantua 2000, Hollowed et al. 2001, Stachura et al. 2014), but the responses of diverse marine communities are not well understood.  These climate-driven perturbations can have impacts at multiple spatial scales as can other disturbances such as oil spills.  One of the largest marine oil spills on record, the 1989 Exxon Valdez oil spill (EVOS) impacted shoreline and shelf areas in a large portion of the GOA region, and threatened rich fisheries and cultural resources.
