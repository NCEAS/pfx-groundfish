Spatial Diversity

nMDS indicated spatial turnover in community composition in both shallow and deep study areas along a longitudinal gradient (Fig. 4). For both shallow and deep areas, PERMANOVA indicated that between-area differences were significant (Shallow: p = 0.005, R\(^2\)\(^{ }\) = 0.222; Deep: p = 0.005, R\(^2\)\(^{ }\) = 0.23) and a test of homogeneity of within-area dispersions affirmed that PERMANOVA results were attributable to real between-area differences in community composition (Shallow: p = 0.94, Deep: p = 0.89).
For either results or discussion: For shallow areas, PERMANOVA indicated that although both spatial and temporal processes had significant effects on community composition, between-area variation was greater than temporal variation in community composition (F = 42.8 and 25.4, respectively). For deep areas, between-area variation had similar importance to temporal variation (F = 27.3 and 24.4, respectively).

(For both shallow and deep study areas, 3/4 of the most dominant species were top predators (Shallow areas: Arrowtooth flounder, Pacific halibut, Pollock (not a predator), and Pacific cod; Deep areas: Arrowtooth flounder, Pacific ocean perch, Pollock, and Sablefish). When only the most dominant species were allowed to contribute to the ordination (ie substituting a Hellinger transformation for a wisconsin-square root transformation), spatial turnover was not apparent among eastern shallow areas (regions 1-7) and diminished among eastern deep areas (regions 1-4). This suggests that, among eastern areas, spatial turnover in community composition was driven by subdominant (but not dominant) species) - in other words, top predators showed spatial stability while subdominant species (a mix of predator and prey species) did not.)

(Spatial stability in) Alpha and Beta diversity

Alpha and beta diversity were remarkably similar across shallow areas (Figure 3x,y ). Our estimates of these diversity components were similar across deep areas as well, although the western portion of Shelikof Strait (area 3) differed somewhat from the other deep areas (Figure 3x,y). Species richness was consistent among all shallow areas east of Kodiak Island (area1 1-7), but was highest in areas west of Kodiak Island (areas 8-9). In the deep areas, species richness was highest along Kodiak Island and west of the Island (areas 3-5) and lowest in the Shelikof Strait and east of Kodiak Island (areas 1 and 2).


(Spatial stability in) Functional diversity

Rao's Q was similar among most shallow and deep study areas (Fig. 3f,g), with the exception of a few shallow areas: a Tukey test indicated significant pairwise differences between shallow areas 6-1, 6-2, 6-4, and 9-2 (p < 0.05). For deep study areas, a 1-way ANOVA indicated no significant differences in Rao's Q between areas (p = 0.071).
