Discussion


Overall, we found that taxanomic and functional diversity varied little at either local or regional spatial scales across the GOA large marine ecosystem.  Species richness showed some variation longitudinally, while alpha diversity (exp\(H\)) was consistently low, and beta diversity (\(H\beta\)) and functional diversity (Rao's Q) were consistently high across the 10 study areas.  Community composition also varied longitudinally, driven by turnover of lower-biomass species.  However, disturbance (fishing pressure) varied unimodally only with local diversity (species richness and exp\(H\)).  Thus, despite differences in species traits, disturbance, community composition, and environmental variation, diversity and community structure were stable across this large marine ecosystem.  This spatial stability appears to have been largely driven by environmental heterogeneity (species sorting), functional redundancy, and disturbance (fishing pressure). 

Diversity
Despite variation in species richness and community composition across local communities, our metrics of diversity and community composition indicate that community structure is conserved across this large marine ecosystem.  We found statistically invariant (do we know this?) alpha diversity, i.e. local communities had a similar degree of skew in relative abundance among those species present in the community.  This result is corroborated by our metric of community structure (either Ole’s triangle figure (so far only for shallow areas) or we could plot ratios of predator:prey abundance for each local community), which indicated that local communities are consistently dominated by apex predators****, with similar ratios of predator : prey abundance.
Functional diversity, computed from traits representing multiple uncorrelated elements of community structure, was invariant, suggesting that local communities were structured similarly with respect to functional roles and contained species that were functionally redundant.

Intriguingly, we found that spatial turnover in community composition occured among subdominant (Fig. Z c, d) but not dominant (Fig. Z a, b) species. The degree of turnover varied somewhat between our study areas, and was strongest in areas 1 - 7 and weaker towards sites 8 - 10 as evidenced by reduced distances between centroids following the Hellinger transformation (see Supp Figs). In other words, identity and community composition of numerically dominant species (Arrowtooth flounder, Pacific halibut, Walleye pollock, Pacific cod) was invariant among eastern sites, and less variable than subdominant species among westernmost sites.  Instead, changes in community composition occurred among subdominant species along the longitudinal (environmental) gradient.  This result, combined with invariance in functional diversity and predator-prey ratios, suggests that there was functional redundancy among sub-dominant species as they were sequentially replaced along the longitudinal (environmental) gradient, i.e. subdominant species were replaced along the longitudinal gradient in such a way that they filled the same community roles in each local community.
