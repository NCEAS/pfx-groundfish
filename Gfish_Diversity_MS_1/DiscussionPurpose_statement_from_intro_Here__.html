Purpose statement from intro:
Here we evaluate the spatial structure of diversity among the assemblage of the 52 most common groundfish species across the Gulf of Alaska. We assess the degree of between-local area differences in (i) species richness, (ii) alpha diversity, (iii) functional diversity, and (iv) the degree to which local areas are saturated relative to the regional species pool (multiplicative beta diversity). We also assess the degree of spatial turnover in community composition. Finally, we evaluate evidence for a spatial portfolio effect: we assess how temporal variation in community abundance scales between local areas and the regional metacommunity.) 

Main results; the spatial structure of diversity:
- species richness, alpha, beta (re. saturation), and functional diversity are conserved across space
- local communities show low (and statistically consistent?) saturation relative to the regional species pool: among shallow areas, local communities held on average 1/7th to 1/10th of the regional species pool; among deep areas, local communities held on average 1/4th to 1/6th of the regional species pool (Figs 2x & y).
- shallow areas were more diverse than deep areas (Bootstrapped gamma (expected gamma) for shallow areas = 56.4-81.6; Bootstrapped gamma for deep areas = 27.4-39.5)
- there is significant spatial turnover in community composition. This turnover occurs among numerically sub-dominant species and less so among numerically dominant species. Turnover occurs along a longitudinal gradient.
- there is evidence for a spatial portfolio effect: for both shallow and deep communities, the regional community showed reduced variation in community abundance relative to local communities. Scaling is >1, indicating a stabilizing (ie portfolio) effect of ...

b) Spatial turnover of community composition
[MH: Here is a good place to cite the Certain and Plangue paper and their findings on the importance of measuring beta diversity].

Low alpha and and low saturation (ie high beta diversity) of local communities likely arose from high spatial turnover of community composition, which in turn likely arose from a longitudinal gradient in environmental conditions (environmental heterogeneity):

- nMDS shows community compositional turnover corresponds to a longitudinal gradient: this suggests that species sorting (Whittaker 1962, Leibold et al. 2004) along an environmental gradient is the mechanism underlying spatial turnover in community composition ... in other words, environmental heterogeneity is more important than dispersal in structuring our local communities despite the fact that this is a completely open system, linked by the strong ACC, and these are mobile species ... remarkable. (consider citing ﻿﻿﻿﻿﻿﻿Whittaker 1962, ﻿﻿﻿﻿﻿﻿Leibold 2004 for context)  Is there precedent for this in an open system with mobile species?
NB: The fact that saturation is low (ie high beta) necessarily means that there will be high spatial turnover in species composition, which underlies the spatial portfoltio effect ... however, note that this is a chicken & egg game: does high beta (low local saturation) give rise to high spatial turnover, or vice versa? 

Most abundant species in shallow areas: Arrowtooth flounder, Pacific halibut, Walleye pollock, Pacific cod. Deep: Arrowtooth flounder, Pacific ocean perch, Walleye pollock, Sablefish
'Redundancy' (replacement) among subdominant species. In shallow areas 1-7 east of Kodiak (and to a lesser degree in deep areas 1-4), compositional turnover was driven by rare and intermediate-density species; community composition with respect to the most abundant species did not show spatial turnover: compositional turnover was not apparent when data were transformed to emphasize the influence of the most abundant species (i.e. Hellinger transformation; Figs. 6 c & d); instead, turnover was only apparent when data were transformed to allow subdominant species equal chance of explaining community structure (i.e. Wisconsin - Square root transformation; Figs. 6 a & b). In other words, all the spatial turnover is going on at the level of less abundant lower trophic-level species, while the most abundant species (here, top predators) show similar spatial composition (we could check if this is supported by those heat maps Ole created last year).

For shallow areas, PERMANOVA indicated that although both spatial and temporal processes had significant effects on community composition, between-area variation was greater than temporal variation in community composition (F = 42.8 and 25.4, respectively). For deep areas, between-area variation had similar importance to temporal variation (F = 27.3 and 24.4, respectively).
